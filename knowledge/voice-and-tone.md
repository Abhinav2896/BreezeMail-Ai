# Voice and Tone Guide

## Professional Tone

Professional tone conveys authority, competence, and respect. It is the default for business correspondence.

### Characteristics
- Clear, direct sentences with minimal filler words
- Formal vocabulary without being stiff ("Please find attached" → "I've attached")
- Active voice preferred over passive where possible
- Third-person references to organizations ("Our team will…" rather than "We guys will…")
- Measured punctuation — no exclamation marks in the body; one is acceptable in a closing

### Example Paragraph
> Thank you for your inquiry regarding the Q3 project timeline. Based on our current resource allocation, we anticipate delivering the first milestone by September 15. I've outlined the key deliverables below for your review.

### Anti-Patterns
- Overly stiff: "It has come to my attention that the aforementioned deliverable…"
- Too casual: "Hey! Just wanted to shoot you a quick note about…"
- Jargon-heavy without explanation: "We'll leverage synergies to optimize the runway…"

## Friendly Tone

Friendly tone is warm and approachable while remaining respectful. Ideal for colleagues, familiar clients, and internal communications.

### Characteristics
- Conversational sentence structure with occasional contractions ("I'm," "we've," "you'll")
- Warm openers that acknowledge the person ("Hope your week is going well!")
- Moderate use of exclamation marks (1–2 per email, never in the subject)
- Inclusive language ("Let's figure this out together")
- Light humor is welcome but never at someone's expense

### Example Paragraph
> I hope you're having a great week! I wanted to follow up on our conversation about the new onboarding flow. The team has made some exciting progress, and I'd love to walk you through the latest updates whenever you have a few minutes.

### Anti-Patterns
- Over-familiar: "OMG you're going to LOVE this!!"
- Sycophantic: "I just think you're doing such an amazing, incredible job…"
- Fake warmth: "Hope this email finds you well!" (overused, feels hollow)

## Casual Tone

Casual tone is relaxed and direct. Suitable for close colleagues, startup culture, and informal updates.

### Characteristics
- Short, punchy sentences
- Contractions are standard ("don't," "can't," "won't")
- First-person plural ("we," "us") builds team identity
- Sentence fragments are acceptable for emphasis ("Quick update.")
- Emoji can appear sparingly in internal emails (never in external)

### Example Paragraph
> Quick heads up — we pushed the new dashboard to staging last night. Looks pretty solid so far. Give it a spin when you get a chance and let me know what you think. No rush.

### Anti-Patterns
- Too terse to the point of rudeness: "Do this. Now."
- Slang that alienates: "Gonna yeet this into prod real quick"
- Excessive emoji: "Hey! 👋😄 Got the update done ✅🎉🔥"

## Apologetic Tone

Apologetic tone expresses genuine regret, takes responsibility, and offers a clear path forward. Critical for customer service, mistake acknowledgment, and conflict resolution.

### Characteristics
- Lead with acknowledgment of the issue, not an excuse
- Use "I/we" statements to take ownership ("We made an error" not "Errors were made")
- Provide a concrete next step or remedy
- Keep the apology proportional — don't over-apologize for minor things
- Close on a forward-looking note

### Example Paragraph
> I sincerely apologize for the delay in processing your order. This fell through the cracks on our end, and I understand how frustrating that must be. I've personally escalated your case, and you should see the shipment confirmation within 24 hours. Please don't hesitate to reach out if there's anything else I can do.

### Anti-Patterns
- Blame-shifting: "Unfortunately, due to circumstances beyond our control…"
- Over-apologizing: "I'm so, so, so sorry, I feel terrible, this is unforgivable…"
- No remedy: apologizing without explaining what happens next

## Assertive Tone

Assertive tone is confident, clear, and action-oriented. Used when decisions need to be communicated firmly, deadlines enforced, or boundaries set — without being aggressive.

### Characteristics
- Direct statements with clear expectations ("I need the report by Friday at 5 PM")
- Minimal hedging language (avoid "maybe," "sort of," "I was just wondering if perhaps…")
- Confident but respectful — never threatening or condescending
- Facts-first structure: state the situation, then the required action
- One clear call to action per email

### Example Paragraph
> After reviewing the current timeline, I've decided to move the launch date to March 1. This gives the QA team the buffer they need without impacting the marketing rollout. Please update your project plans accordingly and confirm by end of day Thursday.

### Anti-Patterns
- Aggressive: "This is unacceptable and I demand an explanation immediately."
- Passive-aggressive: "As per my last email…" / "Just circling back for the third time…"
- Wishy-washy: "I was kind of thinking maybe we could possibly consider…"
