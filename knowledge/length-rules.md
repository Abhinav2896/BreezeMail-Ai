# Email Length Rules

## Short Emails (60–100 words)

### Characteristics
- 2–3 short paragraphs
- No bullet points (or 2–3 at most)
- Single-purpose: one topic, one ask
- Direct and efficient — respect the reader's time

### When to Use
- Quick confirmations ("Confirmed for Tuesday at 2 PM.")
- Simple follow-ups
- Brief status updates
- Yes/no decisions

### Structure Template
1. **Purpose** (1 sentence): Why you're writing
2. **Detail** (1–2 sentences): Key information
3. **Close** (1 sentence): Next step or sign-off

### Example
> Hi Sarah,
>
> Just confirming our meeting for Tuesday at 2 PM in Conference Room B. I'll bring the updated project timeline for review.
>
> See you then!
> Alex

## Medium Emails (120–200 words)

### Characteristics
- 3–4 paragraphs
- Bullet points welcome for lists
- Can cover 1–2 related topics
- Balanced between brevity and thoroughness

### When to Use
- Project updates with context
- Requests that need justification
- Introductions and connection emails
- Feedback with examples

### Structure Template
1. **Purpose** (1–2 sentences): Why you're writing
2. **Context** (2–3 sentences): Background the reader needs
3. **Details/List** (bullets or paragraph): Key points
4. **Call to action** (1 sentence): What you need from them

### Example
> Hello David,
>
> I'm writing to request approval for a $5,000 budget increase for the Q3 marketing campaign. Based on our mid-quarter analysis, the current allocation won't cover the additional social media push we discussed.
>
> Specifically, we need:
> - $2,500 for targeted LinkedIn ads
> - $1,500 for content creation (3 blog posts + graphics)
> - $1,000 for A/B testing tools
>
> Could you review and approve by Thursday? Happy to walk through the projections on a quick call if that's helpful.
>
> Best regards,
> Alex

## Long Emails (250–400 words)

### Characteristics
- 4–6 paragraphs
- Bullet points encouraged for scannability
- Can cover multiple related topics or a complex single topic
- Thorough but still focused — not a novel

### When to Use
- Detailed project proposals
- Comprehensive status reports
- Complex instructions or onboarding
- Multi-point feedback or review

### Structure Template
1. **Executive summary** (2 sentences): Bottom line up front
2. **Context** (1 paragraph): Background
3. **Details** (2–3 paragraphs + bullets): The substance
4. **Next steps** (bullets): Clear action items with owners/deadlines
5. **Close** (1 sentence): Invitation to discuss or confirm

### Important Rules for Long Emails
- **Never exceed 400 words** — if you need more, use an attachment
- **Use headers or bold text** to create visual breaks
- **Front-load the most important information** — assume the reader may only read the first paragraph
- **End with a clear, scannable next-steps section**
- **Don't pad** — every sentence should earn its place

---

## Length Selection Heuristic

| Signal | Recommended Length |
|---|---|
| Single question or confirmation | Short |
| Update with some context needed | Medium |
| Proposal, report, or complex request | Long |
| Apology (minor) | Short |
| Apology (significant) | Medium |
| First contact / introduction | Medium |
| Urgent action needed | Short (get to the point fast) |
