# Language-Specific Writing Hints

## English

### Style
- Use clear, standard English grammar
- Avoid idioms that may confuse non-native readers in international contexts
- American English is the default unless the user specifies British English
- Spell out abbreviations on first use

### Common Pitfalls
- "Kindly do the needful" — avoid; use "Please [specific action]" instead
- "Revert back" — redundant; "reply" or "respond" is clearer
- "Prepone" — not standard English; use "move up" or "reschedule earlier"

## Hinglish

### Style
- Hinglish blends Hindi and English naturally in one sentence
- Use Roman script for Hindi words (no Devanagari in email)
- Keep technical and business terms in English
- Use Hindi for emotional warmth, relationship-building, and softening

### Tone Calibration
- Professional Hinglish: mostly English with Hindi connectors ("Aapka order process ho chuka hai. Please check your email for confirmation.")
- Friendly Hinglish: balanced mix ("Hey! Kaise ho? Just wanted to share the update — sab kuch track pe hai!")
- Casual Hinglish: Hindi-dominant with English nouns ("Yaar, meeting ka time change ho gaya hai. New time 3 PM hai, okay?")

### Greetings in Hinglish
- "Namaste [Name] ji," — respectful, warm
- "Hi [Name]," — neutral, safe
- "Hello ji," — when name is unknown, warm

### Sign-Offs in Hinglish
- "Dhanyavaad," / "Shukriya," — "Thank you" in Hindi/Urdu
- "Best regards," — English sign-off is perfectly normal in Hinglish emails

## Spanish

### Style
- Use formal "usted" for professional contexts; "tú" for friendly/casual
- Spanish sentences tend to be longer; this is natural, don't force English brevity
- Gender agreement matters — match adjectives and pronouns correctly
- Use opening exclamation/question marks: ¡ and ¿

### Greetings in Spanish
- Professional: "Estimado/a [Name]," / "Apreciado/a [Name],"
- Friendly: "Hola [Name]," / "¡Hola!"
- Casual: "¡Hola!" / "¿Qué tal?"

### Sign-Offs in Spanish
- Professional: "Atentamente," / "Cordialmente,"
- Friendly: "Un saludo," / "¡Saludos!"
- Casual: "¡Hasta pronto!" / "Un abrazo,"

## French

### Style
- Use "vous" for professional/formal; "tu" for casual
- French business email tends toward formality — err on the formal side
- Accents matter (é, è, ê, ç, à) — always include them
- The closing formula in French is traditionally elaborate

### Greetings in French
- Professional: "Madame, Monsieur," / "Cher/Chère [Name],"
- Friendly: "Bonjour [Name]," / "Salut [Name],"
- Casual: "Salut!" / "Coucou,"

### Sign-Offs in French
- Professional: "Cordialement," / "Veuillez agréer mes salutations distinguées,"
- Friendly: "Bien cordialement," / "Amicalement,"
- Casual: "À bientôt !" / "Bises,"

## German

### Style
- Use "Sie" for professional/formal; "du" for casual (increasingly common in tech)
- German compound nouns are written as one word (Projektbesprechung, not Projekt Besprechung)
- Capitalize all nouns, not just proper nouns
- Commas before subordinate clauses are mandatory

### Greetings in German
- Professional: "Sehr geehrte/r [Name]," / "Sehr geehrte Damen und Herren,"
- Friendly: "Liebe/r [Name]," / "Hallo [Name],"
- Casual: "Hi [Name]," / "Hallo!"

### Sign-Offs in German
- Professional: "Mit freundlichen Grüßen," / "Hochachtungsvoll,"
- Friendly: "Viele Grüße," / "Liebe Grüße,"
- Casual: "Grüße," / "Bis bald,"

## General Multilingual Guidance

- When writing in a non-English language, maintain the email structure (subject, greeting, body, sign-off) — it's universal
- Don't over-translate idioms; adapt them to the target language's natural expressions
- Keep proper nouns, brand names, and technical terms in their original language
- When unsure about formality level, default to more formal — it's easier to be forgiven for excess formality than for casualness
