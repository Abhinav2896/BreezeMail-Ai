# Common Email Writing Mistakes

## Vague or Generic Greetings

### The Problem
Opening with "Hope this email finds you well" or "To whom it may concern" signals that the writer didn't think about the recipient.

### Better Approach
- Use the recipient's name whenever possible
- If you don't know the name, use their role: "Dear Support Team,"
- Replace hollow openers with purpose: "I'm writing to follow up on our Tuesday meeting."

---

## Walls of Text

### The Problem
Long, unbroken paragraphs are impossible to scan. Recipients skip them, miss key information, and feel overwhelmed.

### Better Approach
- Break content into paragraphs of 2–4 sentences
- Use bullet points for lists of 3+ items
- Add white space between sections
- Bold key terms or deadlines if your email client supports it
- Lead with the most important information

### Example
❌ Bad:
> I wanted to reach out to you about the project timeline because we've been running into some issues with the vendor deliverables and the QA team has flagged several bugs that need to be addressed before we can proceed with the launch and additionally the marketing team needs at least two weeks to prepare the campaign assets so I think we should push the deadline to the end of the month.

✅ Good:
> I'm writing to propose extending the project deadline to March 31. Three issues are driving this:
>
> - Vendor deliverables are delayed by one week
> - QA has flagged 12 bugs that need fixes before launch
> - Marketing needs 2 weeks for campaign asset preparation

## Emoji Overuse

### The Problem
Excessive emoji undermines credibility and makes professional emails feel unprofessional. They also render differently across email clients.

### Guidelines
- **Professional/Assertive emails**: Zero emoji
- **Friendly emails**: 0–1 emoji, only in informal contexts
- **Casual emails**: 1–2 emoji maximum, never in the subject line for business email
- **Apologetic emails**: Zero emoji (they trivialize the apology)

## Burying the Lede

### The Problem
Starting with background context before stating the purpose forces the reader to hunt for the point.

### Better Approach
- State your purpose in the first sentence
- Provide context only after the reader knows why they should care
- Use the "Bottom Line Up Front" (BLUF) technique

### Example
❌ Bad:
> As you may recall, we had a meeting last Thursday where we discussed several agenda items including the budget, staffing, and the upcoming product launch. During that meeting, I mentioned that I would follow up with a proposal...

> I'd like to propose a $15K budget increase for the product launch. Here's the rationale from our Thursday discussion:

## Missing or Unclear Call to Action

### The Problem
Emails that inform without directing leave the reader wondering "What am I supposed to do with this?"

### Better Approach
- End with a clear, specific ask: "Please reply by Friday with your feedback."
- If no action is needed, say so: "No response needed — just keeping you in the loop."
- Limit to one primary call to action per email

---

## Passive Voice Overuse

### The Problem
Passive voice ("The report was completed") hides responsibility and feels bureaucratic.

### Better Approach
- Prefer active voice: "I completed the report" / "The team completed the report"
- Passive is acceptable when the doer is genuinely unknown or irrelevant
- Passive is especially harmful in apologetic emails: "Mistakes were made" → "We made a mistake"

---

## Over-Apologizing

### The Problem
Excessive apologies ("I'm so sorry to bother you, I know you're busy, I hate to ask but…") undermine confidence and waste the reader's time.

### Better Approach
- Apologize once, clearly, for things that warrant an apology
- Replace unnecessary apologies with gratitude: "Sorry for the delay" → "Thank you for your patience"
- Don't apologize for writing an email — that's what email is for

---

## Tone Mismatch

### The Problem
Using a casual tone in a formal context (or vice versa) creates friction and can damage relationships.

### Common Mismatches
- First email to a new client using "Hey!" instead of "Hello [Name],"
- Apology email using casual tone: "Oops, my bad about the invoice!"
- Internal team email using overly formal language: "I hereby request your attendance…"

### Better Approach
- Match tone to the relationship, context, and purpose
- When in doubt, start slightly more formal and mirror the recipient's style
- Reread your email imagining you're the recipient

---

## Forgetting the Subject Line

### The Problem
Empty or vague subject lines ("Hi," "Quick question") get lost in inboxes and reduce open rates.

### Better Approach
- Write the subject line LAST, after the email body is complete
- Make it specific and actionable (see subject-lines.md for patterns)
- Never send an email with an empty subject
