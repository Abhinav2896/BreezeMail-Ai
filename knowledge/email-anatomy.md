# Email Anatomy and Structure

## Subject Line

The subject line is the single most important line of any email. It determines whether the email gets opened, ignored, or lost.

### Rules
- Keep it under 60 characters (mobile truncation threshold)
- Be specific and concrete: "Q3 Budget Proposal — Review by Friday" beats "Quick question"
- Never use all-caps or excessive punctuation
- Front-load the most important word (action or topic)
- Avoid vague subjects: "Update," "Hello," "FYI," "Important"

### Pattern Templates
- **Action request**: "[Action needed] [Topic] by [Deadline]"
- **Information share**: "[Topic]: [Key detail]"
- **Follow-up**: "Following up: [Original topic]"
- **Introduction**: "Introduction: [Person A] ↔ [Person B]"

## Greeting

The greeting sets the tone for the entire email. Match it to the relationship and context.

### Best Practices
- Use the recipient's name when known
- Match formality to the tone (see voice-and-tone.md)
- For unknown recipients, "Hello" or "Hi there" is safe
- Never use "To whom it may concern" in modern business email
- "Dear [Name]," is appropriate for formal/professional contexts

## Body

The body is where the message lives. Structure it for scannability.

### Structure Rules
- **Lead with the purpose** — state why you're writing in the first sentence
- **One idea per paragraph** — keep paragraphs to 2–4 sentences max
- **Use white space** — blank lines between paragraphs improve readability
- **Front-load key information** — don't bury the lede
- **Use transition words** — "Additionally," "However," "To clarify," guide the reader

### When to Use Paragraphs vs. Bullets
- Use **paragraphs** for narrative context, explanations, and relationship-building
- Use **bullets** for:
  - Lists of 3+ items
  - Action items or deliverables
  - Specifications or requirements
  - Comparisons

### Paragraph Length by Email Length
| Email Length | Target Paragraphs | Words per Paragraph |
|---|---|---|
| Short | 2–3 | 15–30 |
| Medium | 3–4 | 25–45 |
| Long | 4–6 | 30–60 |

## Bullet Points

Bullets are powerful tools for clarity. Use them intentionally.

### Rules
- Start each bullet with a consistent grammatical form (all verbs, all nouns, etc.)
- Keep bullets parallel in structure
- Use 3–7 bullets; fewer than 3 → use a sentence; more than 7 → group into categories
- Don't end bullets with periods unless they are complete sentences
- Indent sub-bullets sparingly (one level max in emails)

### When NOT to Use Bullets
- When conveying empathy or emotion (bullets feel cold)
- When the content is naturally narrative
- In apology emails (the structure feels dismissive)
- When there are only 1–2 items (just write a sentence)

## Sign-Off

The sign-off is the last impression. Make it match the tone and end cleanly.

### Best Practices
- Keep it to one line plus your name
- Match formality to the rest of the email
- Don't introduce new information in the sign-off
- A sign-off like "Best regards,\nYour Name" is universally safe
- For casual emails, "Cheers," "Thanks," or just "— [Name]" works well

### Common Sign-Offs by Tone
| Tone | Recommended | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Professional | "Best regards," / "Sincerely," | "Cheers!" / "XOXO" |
| Friendly | "Best," / "Thanks so much!" | "Yours truly," |
| Casual | "Cheers," / "Talk soon," | "Respectfully yours," |
| Apologetic | "Sincerely," / "With apologies," | "Cheers!" |
| Assertive | "Regards," / "Best," | "Humbly," |
